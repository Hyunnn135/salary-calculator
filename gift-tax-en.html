Gift Tax Key Points

10-Year Cumulative Taxation: Gift tax is assessed on cumulative gifts to the same person within 10 years. Exemption limits are also applied on a 10-year basis.

Using Exemption Limits: Gifts to a spouse are tax-free up to 600 million KRW, and to adult children up to 50 million KRW. Early gifting can maximize tax savings.

Voluntary Filing Discount: If you file within 3 months from the end of the month in which the gift was made, you can receive a 3% deduction on the calculated tax.

Timing of Gifts: For assets expected to appreciate like real estate, earlier gifting is more advantageous.

FAQ

How much tax if parents gift 100 million KRW to an adult child?
After deducting the 50 million KRW exemption, the remaining 50 million KRW is taxed at 10%. With voluntary filing, you would pay approximately 4.85 million KRW in gift tax.
How many years does the gift tax exemption limit cover?
The exemption limit is cumulative over 10 years per person. For example, if you received 30 million KRW 5 years ago, only 20 million KRW remains tax-free this time.
Do I need to report cash gifts?
Yes. All gifts of property — including cash, real estate, and stocks — must be reported and taxed if they exceed the exemption limit.
When is the gift tax filing deadline?
You must file at the competent tax office within 3 months from the end of the month in which the gift was received. Filing on time qualifies you for a 3% tax deduction.

Gift Tax Exemptions & Tax Saving

Gift tax is a tax imposed when receiving property from others for free. It is the most commonly encountered tax when transferring assets between family members. Understanding exemption limits and tax rate structures enables legal tax planning.

Gift Tax Exemption Limits (10-Year Cumulative)

Gift tax exemption limits vary by the relationship between donor and recipient, and are applied cumulatively over 10 years. Gifts from a spouse are exempt up to 600 million KRW; from parents to adult children (or grandchildren) up to 50 million KRW; to minor children up to 20 million KRW; from direct ascendants (parents/grandparents) up to 50 million KRW; and from other relatives (blood relatives within 6th degree, in-laws within 4th degree) up to 10 million KRW. Gift tax is imposed on amounts exceeding these limits.

Gift Tax Rates

Gift tax rates are applied progressively based on the taxable base (gift property value minus exemption limit). The rates are: up to 100 million KRW at 10%, 100–500 million KRW at 20%, 500 million–1 billion KRW at 30%, 1–3 billion KRW at 40%, and over 3 billion KRW at 50%. Each bracket has a progressive deduction amount applied when calculating the actual tax.

Tax Saving Strategies Through Early Gifting

Since exemption limits reset every 10 years, spreading gifts to children over time can maximize tax savings. For example, gifting 20 million KRW at birth, another 20 million KRW at age 11, and 50 million KRW at age 21 allows transferring a total of 90 million KRW tax-free. Using debt-inclusive gifts (where liabilities are included in the gift) can convert the debt portion to capital gains tax, potentially reducing overall gift tax.

Filing Gift Tax and Precautions

When gifting real estate, market value appraisal is the principle, and actual transaction prices apply for apartments. Filing within 3 months from the end of the month of the gift qualifies you for a 3% filing discount, making timely filing important. Gift tax returns can be filed electronically through the National Tax Service (Hometax). For high-value or real estate gifts, consulting a tax professional is recommended.

Gift Presumption and Fund Source Investigation

The National Tax Service may investigate the source of funds when property acquisitions seem excessive relative to income. Especially when purchasing real estate or large deposits, failure to prove the source of funds may result in the amount being presumed as a gift and taxed accordingly. Even when borrowing from parents, a written loan agreement and interest payment records are needed for it to be recognized as a loan. Interest-free or below-market loans may be treated as gifts based on a 4.6% annual interest standard. Therefore, it is essential to keep proper documentation for all monetary transactions between family members.
