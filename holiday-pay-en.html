What is Weekly Holiday Pay?

Weekly holiday pay is one day's worth of paid leave allowance given to workers who work 15+ hours per week and have perfect attendance for their scheduled work days. It applies equally to full-time, contract, and part-time workers.

No Holiday Pay

Working under 15 hrs/week
Absent on a scheduled work day

Holiday Pay Applies

Working 15+ hrs/week
Perfect attendance on scheduled days

Holiday Pay Formula
Under 40 hrs/week: Hourly Wage × (Weekly Hours / 40) × 8
40+ hrs/week: Hourly Wage × 8 hrs (max cap)

What Happens if Holiday Pay is Not Paid?

If an employer fails to pay weekly holiday pay, they may face up to 2 years imprisonment or a fine of up to 20 million KRW (Article 110 of the Labor Standards Act).

If you haven't received holiday pay, you can report it to the Ministry of Employment and Labor hotline (1350) or file a complaint at your local labor office.

FAQ

Do I get holiday pay if I work exactly 15 hours/week?
Yes, holiday pay applies for 15 hours or more per week. 15 hours is included.
Are hours combined if I work at multiple places?
No. Hours are calculated separately for each workplace. Even if you work 10 hrs at Store A + 10 hrs at Cafe B, if each is under 15 hrs, there is no holiday pay for either.
Is holiday pay given during probation?
Yes. If you work 15+ hours/week during probation, holiday pay applies. Employers cannot withhold it because of probation status.
Does being late or leaving early affect holiday pay?
Being late or leaving early is not considered an absence, so it does not affect holiday pay. Only when you are completely absent on a scheduled work day will that week's holiday pay be forfeited.
Is it illegal for a company not to pay holiday pay?
Yes. Failure to pay holiday pay violates the Labor Standards Act, punishable by up to 2 years imprisonment or a fine of up to 20 million KRW. You can report it to the Ministry of Employment and Labor (1350).
Is holiday pay taxed?
Yes. Holiday pay is part of wages and is subject to income tax and social insurance contributions.

Detailed Guide to Weekly Holiday Pay Standards and Calculation

Weekly holiday pay is based on Article 55 of the Labor Standards Act, which requires employers to grant a paid weekly holiday to workers with perfect attendance during their scheduled work week. Simply put, if you work faithfully for a week, you receive an extra day's pay.

Eligibility Requirements

To receive holiday pay, two conditions must be met. First, scheduled weekly working hours must be 15 hours or more. Second, you must have perfect attendance for all scheduled work days that week. An important note: if you average 15+ hours per week over a 4-week period, you may still be eligible for holiday pay even if a specific week falls under 15 hours. However, daily workers and freelancers (contract workers) are often not eligible for holiday pay depending on the nature of their employment contract.

Calculation Formula

The holiday pay formula is (Weekly Scheduled Hours ÷ 40) × 8 × Hourly Wage. For example, if you work 40 hrs/week, the calculation is (40÷40)×8×Hourly Wage, resulting in 8 hours' worth of wages as holiday pay. For a part-time worker working 20 hours/week, the calculation is (20÷40)×8×Hourly Wage, resulting in 4 hours' worth of wages. When you factor in holiday pay, the effective hourly wage for a 40-hour work week becomes approximately 1.2 times the base hourly wage.

What to Do If Holiday Pay Is Not Paid

If an employer fails to pay holiday pay, this violates the Labor Standards Act. First request payment verbally or in writing. If unresolved, contact the Ministry of Employment and Labor (1350) or file a wage complaint at your local labor office. Having your employment contract, attendance records, and pay stubs ready will help speed up the process. The statute of limitations for unpaid wages is 3 years, so act as soon as possible.

Additional FAQ

Can you receive holiday pay for your last week when resigning? If you had perfect attendance for all scheduled work days in the week of your resignation, you are eligible for holiday pay. However, depending on your resignation date, if you did not complete all scheduled work days that week, it may not be paid. Also, public holidays and annual leave days are not considered absences, so they do not affect the perfect attendance requirement. Holiday pay is a fundamental right of workers, so it is important to understand your working conditions and request fair wages.
