What is Income Tax?

Comprehensive income tax is a tax levied on the total combined income an individual earns over one year. It must be filed and paid for the previous year's income annually from May 1 to May 31.

Who needs to file:

  • Freelancers -- Those with business income subject to 3.3% withholding tax
  • Self-employed -- Those who operate a business and generate income
  • Rental income earners -- Those with real estate rental income
  • Other income earners -- Those with other income (lecture fees, manuscript fees, etc.) exceeding 3 million KRW
  • Multiple employers -- Those receiving salary from two or more workplaces

For freelancers, the withheld 3.3% (3% income tax + 0.3% local income tax) is compared with the actual tax amount to determine whether you receive a refund or owe additional payment.

FAQ

Q. I'm a freelancer. Do I have to file income tax?
Yes. Even if 3.3% was withheld, you must file income tax in May. In fact, if your expenses are recognized, your actual tax may be less than what was withheld, resulting in a refund in many cases.
Q. I already had 3.3% withheld. Why do I owe more?
The 3.3% is a provisional withholding tax. If your actual income is high or your expense rate is low, the actual tax may exceed 3.3%, resulting in additional payment.
Q. What is the expense rate?
It is the ratio of expenses incurred to earn income. You can apply the 'standard expense rate' set by the National Tax Service for each industry, or enter your actual expenses directly. The higher the expense rate, the lower your tax.
Q. What happens if I don't file income tax?
A penalty of 20% of the tax due will be imposed for non-filing, plus a late payment penalty. If you are eligible for a refund but don't file, you won't receive it.
Q. When can I receive my refund?
If you file in May, the refund is usually deposited into your account between June and July. Filing through Hometax may speed up processing.
Q. Can I file directly through Hometax?
Yes. You can file income tax on the National Tax Service Hometax website (hometax.go.kr). If a pre-filled return is available, you can complete it in just a few clicks.

Income Tax Saving Guide

Comprehensive income tax is levied on all income an individual earns over one year. For salaried workers with only employment income, tax obligations are settled through year-end tax adjustment. However, if you have additional income such as business income, rental income, or financial income, you must file and pay income tax every May.

Who Needs to File Income Tax

You need to file income tax if you have financial income (interest/dividends) exceeding 20 million KRW annually in addition to employment income, business income, rental income, or other income. Common filers include freelancers (subject to 3.3% withholding), sole proprietors, rental property owners, and individual media creators such as YouTubers and bloggers. The filing period is May 1 to May 31 each year, extended to June 30 for those subject to faithful filing confirmation.

2026 Income Tax Brackets

The tax rates are: up to 14 million KRW at 6%, 14–50 million KRW at 15%, 50–88 million KRW at 24%, 88–150 million KRW at 35%, 150–300 million KRW at 38%, 300–500 million KRW at 40%, 500 million–1 billion KRW at 42%, and over 1 billion KRW at 45%. This is a progressive tax structure where higher taxable bases face higher rates — the top rate does not apply to all income, but each bracket is taxed at its corresponding rate.

Key Deductions and Tax-Saving Strategies

The key to saving on income tax is maximizing deductions. Be thorough with personal exemptions (self, spouse, dependents), pension premium deductions, special income deductions (health insurance premiums, housing funds), credit card income deductions, and donation deductions. For freelancers, applying the correct expense rate is crucial -- choose between simplified bookkeeping and double-entry bookkeeping based on your income level. Income tax can be filed electronically on the National Tax Service Hometax website (hometax.go.kr). For complex cases, consulting a tax professional is recommended.

Penalties for Non-Filing

If you fail to file income tax by the deadline, a non-filing penalty of 20% of the calculated tax is imposed. For fraudulent non-filing, the penalty can reach up to 40%. If you miss the payment deadline, a late payment penalty of 0.022% per day accrues on the unpaid tax. Meanwhile, through the amended return system, you can receive refunds for overpaid taxes due to missed deductions within the past 5 years. Medical expenses, education costs, and donations are commonly overlooked, so it is worth reviewing past filings.
